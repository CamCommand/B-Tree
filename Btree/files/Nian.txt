   <p>According to <a href="/wiki/Chinese_mythology" title="Chinese mythology">Chinese mythology</a>, a <b>Nian</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans">年兽</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant">年獸</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">nián shòu</span></i>) is a beast that lives under the sea or in the mountains. The character <i>Nian</i> more usually means "year" or "new year". The earliest written sources that refer to the <i>Nian</i> as a creature date to early 20th century. As a result, it is unclear whether the <i>Nian</i> creature is an authentic part of traditional folk mythology, or a part of a local oral tradition which was recorded in the early 20th century. <i>Nian</i> is one of the key characters in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a>, with scholars citing it as the reason behind several practices during the celebration such as wearing red clothing and creating noise from drums and <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_legends">Modern legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modern legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Once every year at the beginning of Chinese New Year, the Nian comes out of its hiding place to feed, mostly on men and animals. During winter, since food is sparse, he would go to the village. He would eat the crops and sometimes the villagers, mostly children. There are several accounts as to how it looked, such as the way some sources cited that it resembles a flat-face lion with a dog's body and prominent incisor.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Other authors described it as larger than an elephant with two long horns and many sharp teeth.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The weaknesses of the Nian are purported to be a sensitivity to loud noises, fire, and a fear of the color red. </p>
   <p>Some local legends attribute the <a href="/wiki/Lion_dance" title="Lion dance">Chinese lion dance</a> (Chinese: <span lang="zh-Hant">舞獅</span>) to the legend of the Nian. The tradition has its origins in a story of a Nian's attack on a village. After the attack, the villagers discussed how to make the Nian leave them in peace. Since it was discovered that the beast was afraid of the color red, people would put red lanterns and spring scrolls on their windows and doors.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They would also leave food at their doorstep in a bid to divert it from eating humans. </p>
   <p>The traditions of firecrackers, red lanterns, and red robes found in many Lion Dance portrayals originate from the plan the villagers had in which drums, plates and empty bowls were hit, red robes were worn, and <a href="/wiki/Firecrackers" class="mw-redirect" title="Firecrackers">firecrackers</a> were thrown, causing loud banging sounds that they hoped would intimidate the Nian. According to this same myth, captured by Hongjun Laozu, an ancient <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">Taoist</a> monk, and Nian became Hongjun Laozu's mount. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_sources">Ancient sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Various aspects of cultural practices relating to Chinese New Year is now included as part of the <i>Nian</i> legend. These cultural practices are recorded in ancient texts, though none of them refer to a creature called <i>Nian</i> </p>
   <p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Erya" title="Erya">Erya</a></i> records that the character <i>Nian</i> (年) was first used to mean the year during the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>, replacing the different terms used in previous eras. The <i><a href="/wiki/Shuowen_Jiezi" title="Shuowen Jiezi">Shuowen Jiezi</a></i> records that the character <i>Nian</i> meant "ripeness of grains", and was composed of the character "<i>he</i>" (禾, rice plant) and "<i>qian</i>" (千, indicating the sound), and quotes the <i><a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_Annals" title="Spring and Autumn Annals">Chunqiu</a></i> which uses it in the sense of a great harvest. </p>
   <p>The attributes of the <i>Nian</i> creature in the modern legend, of fear of noise and fire, correlates with ancient legends relating to the use of firecrackers to drive off ape-like creatures in the mountains called <i>shanxiao</i> (山魈), first recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Shanhaijing" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanhaijing">Shanhaijing</a></i>. </p>
   <p>The practice of sweeping and cleaning at the start of the year is recorded in Zhou dynasty sources as intended to ward off plague spirits, and the practice of using <a href="/wiki/Nuo_opera" title="Nuo opera">music and drama</a> to receive gods and ward off plague spirits is also recorded from the same era. The creature's role in the celebration of the Chinese New Year is highlighted by the way the Chinese call this holiday as <i>Guo Nian</i>, which means "pass over <i>Nian</i>" or "overcome <i>Nian</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p>
