   <p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, the <b>shell theorem</b> gives gravitational simplifications that can be applied to objects inside or outside a spherically <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetrical</a> body. This theorem has particular application to <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>. </p>
   <p><a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> proved the shell theorem<sup id="cite_ref-Newton_philo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newton_philo-1">[1]</a></sup> and stated that: </p> 
   <ol>
    <li>A <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spherically</a> <a href="/wiki/Symmetry" title="Symmetry">symmetric</a> body affects external objects gravitationally as though all of its <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> were concentrated at a <a href="/wiki/Point_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Point mass">point</a> at its centre.</li> 
    <li>If the body is a spherically symmetric shell (i.e., a hollow ball), no net <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravitational force">gravitational force</a> is exerted by the shell on any object inside, regardless of the object's location within the shell.</li>
   </ol>

   <p>These results were important to Newton's analysis of planetary motion; they are not immediately obvious, but they can be proven with <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>. (Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity" title="Gauss's law for gravity">Gauss's law for gravity</a> offers a much simpler way to prove the same results.) </p>
   <p>In addition to gravity, the shell theorem can also be used to describe the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> generated by a static spherically symmetric <a href="/wiki/Charge_density" title="Charge density">charge density</a>, or similarly for any other phenomenon that follows an <a href="/wiki/Inverse_square_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverse square law">inverse square law</a>. The derivations below focus on gravity, but the results can easily be generalized to the <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrostatic force">electrostatic force</a>. Moreover, the results can be generalized to the case of general ellipsoidal bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span id="Newton.27s_proofs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Newton's_proofs">Newton's proofs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shell_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Newton's proofs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shell_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Propositions 70 and 71 consider the force acting on a particle from a hollow sphere with an infinitesimally thin surface, whose mass density is constant over the surface. The force on the particle from a small area of the surface of the sphere is proportional to the mass of the area and inversely as the square of its distance from the particle. The first proposition considers the case when the particle is inside the sphere, the second when it is outside. The use of infinitesimals and limiting processes in geometrical constructions are simple and elegant and avoid the need for any integrations. They well illustrate Newton's method of proving many of the propositions in the <i>Principia</i>. </p>
   <p>His proof of Propositions 70 is trivial. In the following, it is considered in slightly greater detail than Newton provides. </p>
   <p>The proof of Proposition 71 is more historically significant. It forms the first part of his proof that the gravitational force of a solid sphere acting on a particle outside it is inversely proportional to the square of its distance from the centre of the sphere, provided the density at any point inside the sphere is a function only of its distance from the centre of the sphere. </p>
   <p>Although the following are completely faithful to Newton's proofs, very minor changes have been made to attempt to make them clearer. </p>

   <p>Since the force of attraction on P in the direction JM from either of the elliptic areas, is direct as the mass of the area and inversely as the square of its distance from P, it is independent of the distance of P from the sphere.    <p>As the position of P and the direction of JM are both arbitrary, it follows that any particle inside a hollow sphere experiences no net force from the mass of the sphere. </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell_theorem_in_general_relativity">Shell theorem in general relativity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shell_theorem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Shell theorem in general relativity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>There is an analog of the shell theorem in <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> (GR). The shell theorem shows that the gravitational potential outside a spherically symmetric body is given by -GM/r—whether or not the body is changing with time. In general relativity, there is a similar theorem that demonstrating that the spacetime geometry <i>outside</i> a spherically symmetric mass-energy distribution is the time-independent Schwarzschild geometry—even if the central mass is undergoing gravitational collapse (Misner et al. 1973). </p>
   <p>This equivalent of the shell theorem in GR is very useful because it allows to use the machinery of the Schwarzschild metric to understand the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_collapse" title="Gravitational collapse">gravitational collapse</a> leading to a black hole, and its effect on the motion of light-rays and particles outside and inside the event horizon (Hartle 2003, chapter 12). </p>
