   <p><b>Wrecking</b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">вредительство</span> or <i>vreditel'stvo</i>, lit. "inflicting damage", "harming"), was a crime specified in the criminal code of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Stalin_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Stalin era">Stalin era</a>. It is often translated as "<a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a>"; however, "wrecking", "diversionist acts", and "counter-revolutionary sabotage" were distinct sub-articles of <a href="/wiki/Article_58_(RSFSR_Penal_Code)" title="Article 58 (RSFSR Penal Code)">Article 58 (RSFSR Penal Code)</a> (58-7, 58-9, and 58-14 respectively), and the meaning of "wrecking" is closer to "undermining". </p>
   <p>These three categories were distinguished in the following way: </p> 
   <ul>
    <li><i>Diversions</i> were acts of immediate infliction of physical damage on state and cooperative property.</li> 
    <li><i>Wrecking</i> was deliberate acts aimed against normal functioning of state and cooperative organisations, such as giving deliberately wrong commands.</li> 
    <li><i>Sabotage</i> was non-execution, or careless execution, of one's duties.</li>
   </ul> 
   <p>As applied in practice, "wrecking" and "sabotage" referred to any action which negatively affected the economy, including failing to meet unrealistic economic targets, allegedly causing poor morale among subordinates (e.g. by complaining about conditions of work), lack of effort, or other incompetence. Thus, it referred to economic or industrial sabotage in the very broadest sense. The definition of sabotage was interpreted <a href="/wiki/Dialectics" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialectics">dialectically</a> and indirectly, so any form of non-compliance with Party directives could have been considered a 'sabotage'.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Volkov_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Volkov-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span id=".22Wrecking.22_in_the_Soviet_Census_of_1937"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Wrecking&quot;_in_the_Soviet_Census_of_1937">"Wrecking" in the Soviet Census of 1937</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wrecking_(Soviet_Union)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: &quot;Wrecking&quot; in the Soviet Census of 1937">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>One of the <a href="/wiki/Show_trial" title="Show trial">show trials</a> involving charges of "wrecking" was that of official coordinators of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Census_(1937)" title="Soviet Census (1937)">1937 Soviet Census</a>. The census was organised with great expectations from the government that it would confirm the superiority of the Soviet economic and social model, with Stalin publicly declaring in 1934 that the Soviet Union was gaining at least 3 million citizens per year. The census questions and procedure were prepared for several years by a commission of professional statisticians and demographers, but then significantly changed by Stalin and other members of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union">Central Committee</a> to better match their political goals – the collection phase of the census was shortened and the procedures simplified. The results obtained from the census were at least 8 million people short of the expected outcome and immediately classified. The formal organizers were accused of "wrecking" and sent to camps, and the shortage was officially presented as caused by their negligence and intentional sabotage. The actual measurement error, as estimated by statisticians in the 1990s, did not exceed 1.5% and such a disappointing result was caused by the huge loss of life that occurred during <a href="/wiki/Collectivization_in_the_Soviet_Union" title="Collectivization in the Soviet Union">collectivization</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_famine_of_1932-1933" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet famine of 1932-1933">Famine of 1932–1933</a>, including in the Ukraine (<a href="/wiki/Holodomor" title="Holodomor">Holodomor</a>), and the mortality rates in the <a href="/wiki/Gulag" title="Gulag">Gulag</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Volkov_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Volkov-3">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_depictions">Cultural depictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wrecking_(Soviet_Union)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultural depictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p><a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Solzhenitsyn" title="Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn">Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn</a>, in his historical account of the prison camps of the Soviet Union, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gulag_Archipelago" title="The Gulag Archipelago">The Gulag Archipelago</a></i>, describes <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_von_Meck" title="Nikolai von Meck">Nikolai von Meck</a> (son of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Otto_Georg_von_Meck" title="Karl Otto Georg von Meck">Karl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nadezhda_von_Meck" title="Nadezhda von Meck">Nadezhda von Meck</a>, patroness of <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Tchaikovsky" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyotr Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>), an engineer who advised heavier-than-average loads being placed on <a href="/wiki/Freight_trains" class="mw-redirect" title="Freight trains">freight trains</a> for the betterment of the economy. He was accused of being a wrecker and shot – his supposed crime being having overloaded the trains for the purpose of wearing out the rails faster.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>In <a href="/wiki/Harry_Turtledove" title="Harry Turtledove">Harry Turtledove</a>'s 2015 <a href="/wiki/Alternate_history" title="Alternate history">alternate history</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Joe_Steele_(novel)" title="Joe Steele (novel)">Joe Steele</a></i>, set in a timeline where Stalin (known as "Joe Steele") was born in the United States and becomes <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> in 1932, "wrecking" becomes a federal crime, resulting in numerous people being imprisoned and in some cases executed for it, with the equivalent of Gulags being located in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Territory_of_Alaska" title="Territory of Alaska">Territory of Alaska</a>. </p>
