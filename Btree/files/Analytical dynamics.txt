   <p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>, <b>analytical dynamics</b>, or more briefly <b>dynamics</b>, is concerned with the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Motion (physics)">motion</a> of bodies and its causes, namely the <a href="/wiki/Force_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Force (physics)">forces</a> acting on the bodies and the properties of the bodies, particularly <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moment of inertia</a>. The foundation of modern-day dynamics is <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">Newtonian mechanics</a> and its reformulation as <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics" title="Lagrangian mechanics">Lagrangian mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics" title="Hamiltonian mechanics">Hamiltonian mechanics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Doran_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doran-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lanczos_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lanczos-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Analytical_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>The field has a long and important history, as remarked by <a href="/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton" title="William Rowan Hamilton">Hamilton</a>: "The theoretical development of the laws of motion of bodies is a problem of such interest and importance that it has engaged the attention of all the eminent mathematicians since the invention of the dynamics as a mathematical science by <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo</a>, and especially since the wonderful extension which was given to that science by <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>." William Rowan Hamilton, 1834 (Transcribed in <i>Classical Mechanics</i> by J.R. Taylor, p.&nbsp;237<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-3">[3]</a></sup>) </p>
   <p>Some authors (for example, Taylor (2005)<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-3">[3]</a></sup> and Greenwood (1997)<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-4">[4]</a></sup>) include <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">special relativity</a> within classical dynamics. </p> 
   <h2><span id="Relationship_to_statics.2C_kinetics.2C_and_kinematics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_statics,_kinetics,_and_kinematics">Relationship to statics, kinetics, and kinematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Analytical_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship to statics, kinetics, and kinematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Historically, there were three branches of <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classical mechanics</a>: </p> 
   <ul>
    <li>"<a href="/wiki/Statics" title="Statics">statics</a>" (the study of equilibrium and its relation to forces)</li> 
    <li>"<a href="/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)" title="Kinetics (physics)">kinetics</a>" (the study of motion and its relation to forces).<sup id="cite_ref-Wright_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wright-5">[5]</a></sup></li> 
    <li>"<a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">kinematics</a>" (dealing with the implications of observed motions without regard for circumstances causing them).<sup id="cite_ref-Whittaker1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whittaker1-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
   </ul> 
   <p>These three subjects have been connected to <i>dynamics</i> in several ways. One approach combined statics and kinetics under the name dynamics, which became the branch dealing with determination of the motion of bodies resulting from the action of specified forces;<sup id="cite_ref-MacGregor_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacGregor-7">[7]</a></sup> another approach separated statics, and combined kinetics and kinematics under the rubric dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-Timoshenko_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timoshenko-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rao1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rao1-9">[9]</a></sup> This approach is common in engineering books on mechanics, and is still in widespread use among mechanicians. </p> 
   <h3><span id="Fundamental_importance_in_engineering.2C_diminishing_emphasis_in_physics"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_importance_in_engineering,_diminishing_emphasis_in_physics">Fundamental importance in engineering, diminishing emphasis in physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Analytical_dynamics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fundamental importance in engineering, diminishing emphasis in physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Today, <i>dynamics</i> and <i>kinematics</i> continue to be considered the two pillars of classical mechanics. Dynamics is still included in mechanical, aerospace, and other engineering curricula because of its importance in machine design, the design of land, sea, air and space vehicles and other applications. However, few modern physicists concern themselves with an independent treatment of "dynamics" or "kinematics," nevermind "statics" or "kinetics." Instead, the entire undifferentiated subject is referred to as <i>classical mechanics</i>. In fact, many undergraduate and graduate text books since mid-20th century on "classical mechanics" lack chapters titled "dynamics" or "kinematics."<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hestenes_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hestenes-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gregory_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gregory-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Landau_et_al._12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Landau_et_al.-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jose_et_al._13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jose_et_al.-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kibble_et_al._14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kibble_et_al.-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Greiner_et_al._15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greiner_et_al.-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sussman_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sussman-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Iro_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Iro-17">[17]</a></sup> In these books, although the word "dynamics" is used when acceleration is ascribed to a force, the word "kinetics" is never mentioned. However, clear exceptions exist. Prominent examples include <i><a href="/wiki/The_Feynman_Lectures_on_Physics" title="The Feynman Lectures on Physics">The Feynman Lectures on Physics</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Feynman_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Feynman-18">[18]</a></sup> </p>
