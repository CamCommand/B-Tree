   <p><b>Intellect</b> is a term used in studies of the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a>, and refers to the ability of the mind to come to correct conclusions about what is <a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">true</a> or <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">real</a>, and about how to <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">solve problems</a>. Historically the term comes from the Greek philosophical term <i>nous</i>, which was translated into Latin as <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intellectus" class="extiw" title="wikt:intellectus">intellectus</a></i> (derived from the verb <i>intelligere</i>, "to understand", from <i>inter</i>, "between" and <i>legere</i>, "to choose"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) and into French and then English as <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intelligence#French" class="extiw" title="wikt:intelligence">intelligence</a></i> (other than <i>intellect</i>). Intellect is in fact considered as a branch of <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligence</a> (see <a href="#Intellect_vs_intelligence">Intellect vs intelligence</a>). </p>
   <p>Discussion of the intellect can be divided into two broad areas. In both of these areas, the terms "intellect" and "intelligence" have continued to be used as related words. </p> 
   <ul>
    <li>In philosophy, especially in <a href="/wiki/Classical_philosophy" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical philosophy">classical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medieval_philosophy" title="Medieval philosophy">medieval philosophy</a> the intellect or <i><a href="/wiki/Nous" title="Nous">nous</a></i> is an important subject connected to the question of how humans can <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">know things</a>. Especially during <a href="/wiki/Late_antiquity" title="Late antiquity">late antiquity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the intellect was often proposed as a concept which could reconcile philosophical and scientific understandings of <a href="/wiki/Nature_(philosophy)" title="Nature (philosophy)">nature</a> with <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheistic</a> religious understandings, by making the intellect a link between each human <a href="/wiki/Soul_(spirit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Soul (spirit)">soul</a>, and the divine intellect (or intellects) of the <a href="/wiki/Cosmos" title="Cosmos">cosmos</a> itself. (During the Latin Middle Ages a distinction developed whereby the term "intelligence" was typically used to refer to the incorporeal beings which governed the celestial spheres in many of these accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-Davidson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davidson-2">[2]</a></sup>) Also see: <a href="/wiki/Passive_intellect" title="Passive intellect">passive intellect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Active_intellect" title="Active intellect">active intellect</a>.</li> 
    <li>In modern <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a>, intelligence and intellect are used as terms describing mental ability (or <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_multiple_intelligences" title="Theory of multiple intelligences">abilities</a>) that allow people to <a href="/wiki/Understanding" title="Understanding">understand</a>. A distinction is sometimes made whereby intellect is considered to be related to "facts" in contrast to intelligence concerning "feelings".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Intellect refers to the cognition and rational mental processes gained through external input rather than internal.</li>
   </ul> 
   <p>A person who uses intelligence (thought and reason) and <a href="/wiki/Critical_thinking" title="Critical thinking">critical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">analytical</a> reasoning in either a <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">professional</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Individual_capacity" title="Individual capacity">personal capacity</a> is often referred to as an <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">intellectual</a>. </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellect_vs_intelligence">Intellect vs intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intellect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Intellect vs intelligence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Intellect is often considered to be a branch of <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligence</a> reflecting mainly its logical and rational side. </p>
   <p>Because of the lack of <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional</a> and sensitive engagement, intellect is sometimes considered to be strictly limited to <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">facts</a> and not going beyond mere raw knowledge.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, intellect can carry a high level of complexity and thus avoid linear and formal logic patterns by referring to such mental processes as, for example, <a href="/wiki/Fuzzy_logic" title="Fuzzy logic">fuzzy logic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dialectical_logic" title="Dialectical logic">dialectical logic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span id="Development_of_intellect.5B6.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_intellect[6]">Development of intellect<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intellect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of intellect[6]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p><a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">Experience</a> plays a crucial role in the formation of intellect. Through solving life problems people can reach intellectual enlightenment and improve their <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavioral</a> patterns to act more reasonably and appropriately in the future. Therefore, intellectual development is encouraged by the feeling of dissatisfaction gained from particular situation’s outcome and the emanating search for better solutions. Only experience can provide people with genuine and thoughtful <a href="/wiki/Understanding" title="Understanding">understanding</a> of <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a>, which consequently contributes to intellectual development. </p>
   <p>Understanding is built upon creating a <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_model" title="Conceptual model">model</a> of the reality through human <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive reflection</a>. The process of finding solutions to life problems enriches human mental “database” of reality attributes. When acquiring the most accurate understanding of the surrounding world, the model mirrored in human <a href="/wiki/Mind" title="Mind">mind</a> becomes very similar or identical to the reality and that is when the intellect releases its full potential. </p>
   <p>Intellect that reaches its maturity<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is referred as self-management. At this state intellect can encounter every problem it faces and not only change itself in the most efficient manner, but also alter the reality in the most desirable way. Overall, success and <a href="/wiki/Failure" title="Failure">failure</a> turn to depend primarily on the extent of intellectual capabilities. </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellect_structure">Intellect structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intellect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Intellect structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>According to <a href="/wiki/J._P._Guilford" title="J. P. Guilford">J.P.Guilford</a>, intellect consists of three key parameters: operations, contents and products. Each parameter contains specific elements, which are independent and therefore should be measured individually and considered as autonomous units of human intellect.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>Operations are represented by <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Divergent_thinking" title="Divergent thinking">divergent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convergent_thinking" title="Convergent thinking">convergent production</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation">evaluation</a>. Content can be figural, <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> and behavioral. Products are divided into units, classes, <a href="/wiki/Relation_of_Ideas" title="Relation of Ideas">relations</a>, systems, transformations and implications.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intellect_in_therapy">Intellect in therapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Intellect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intellect in therapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p><a href="/wiki/Intellectualization" title="Intellectualization">Intellectualization</a> is a therapeutic method, which is based on avoiding emotional penetration through intense intellectual focus. When exercising this technique, the problem that can cause psychological disturbance is approached from rational, cool and scientific attitude. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_mechanisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense mechanisms">defense mechanisms</a> described by <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Freud</a> that blocks <a href="/wiki/Feeling" title="Feeling">feelings</a> to prevent human <a href="/wiki/Psyche_(psychology)" title="Psyche (psychology)">psyche</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stress_(psychological)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress (psychological)">stress</a>. </p>
   <p>The principle that shapes intellectualization involves Freud’s concept of <a href="/wiki/Id,_ego_and_super-ego" title="Id, ego and super-ego">Id, ego and super-ego</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ego_psychology" title="Ego psychology">Ego</a> is a conscious part of <a href="/wiki/Personality" title="Personality">human personality</a>, Id refers to unconscious, “dark” side of human nature driven by animal instincts, and super-ego is a controlling mechanism that adjusts people’s actions and behavior to social norms and expectations. The purpose of intellectualization is to isolate Id and make conscious aspects of life the only object of reflection and consideration. Therefore, this defense mechanism aims to protect ego from unconscious processes, which are often impossible to handle and control.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>One of the ways to launch intellectualization is to use <a href="/wiki/Jargon" title="Jargon">jargon</a> and complex <a href="/wiki/Scientific_terminology" title="Scientific terminology">scientific terms</a> instead of regularly used words. A common example is saying “carcinoma” instead of “cancer” if a doctor wants to mitigate the effect of announcing the disease by driving patient’s attention away from the illness and approaching it more systematically to avoid emotional outburst.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>However, intellectualization is sometimes criticized because of its disregard of feelings. That subsequently leads to the full denial of <a href="/wiki/Intuition" title="Intuition">intuition</a>, which sometimes contributes to <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making process</a>. Moreover, the absence of emotional stimuli can deprive people from <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a> and therefore lead to dissatisfaction and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">melancholy</a>. Furthermore, “emotional constipation” threatens human <a href="/wiki/Creativity" title="Creativity">creativity capabilities</a> by replacing them with more matter-of-fact solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
