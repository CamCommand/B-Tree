   <p>A <b>flettner airplane</b> is a type of rotor airplane which uses a <a href="/wiki/Flettner_rotor" title="Flettner rotor">Flettner rotor</a> to provide lift. The rotor comprises a spinning cylinder with circular end plates and, in an aircraft, spins about a spanwise horizontal axis. When the aircraft moves forward the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a> creates lift.<sup id="cite_ref-popsci1930_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popsci1930-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
   <p><a href="/wiki/Anton_Flettner" title="Anton Flettner">Anton Flettner</a>, after whom the rotor is named, used it successfully as the sails of a ship. He also suggested its use as a wing for a rotor airplane. </p>
   <p>The Butler Ames Aerocycle was built in 1910 and tested on board a warship. There is no record of it having flown.<sup id="cite_ref-popsci1930_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popsci1930-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seifert_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seifert-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plymouth_A-A-2004&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plymouth A-A-2004 (page does not exist)">Plymouth A-A-2004</a> was built for Zaparka in 1930 by three anonymous American inventors, and was reported to have made successful flights over <a href="/wiki/Long_Island_Sound" title="Long Island Sound">Long Island Sound</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-popsci1930_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popsci1930-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-seifert_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-seifert-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>An inherent safety concern is that if power to the rotating drums was lost—even if <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> was maintained—the aircraft would lose its ability to generate lift as the drum slowed down and it would not be able to sustain flight.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p>
