   <p>A <b>mathematical problem</b> is a problem that is amenable to being <a href="/wiki/Representation_(mathematics)" title="Representation (mathematics)">represented</a>, analyzed, and possibly solved, with the methods of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. This can be a real-world problem, such as computing the <a href="/wiki/Orbit#Planetary_orbits" title="Orbit">orbits</a> of the planets in the solar system, or a problem of a more abstract nature, such as <a href="/wiki/Hilbert%27s_problems" title="Hilbert's problems">Hilbert's problems</a>.<br> </p>
   <p>It can also be a problem referring to the <a href="/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics" title="Foundations of mathematics">nature of mathematics</a> itself, such as <a href="/wiki/Russell%27s_Paradox" class="mw-redirect" title="Russell's Paradox">Russell's Paradox</a>. </p>
   <p>The result of mathematical problem solved is <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_proof" title="Mathematical proof">demonstrated</a> and examined formally. </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real-world_problems">Real-world problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Real-world problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Informal "real-world" mathematical problems are questions related to a concrete setting, such as "Adam has five apples and gives John three. How many has he left?". Such questions are usually more difficult to solve than regular <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_exercise" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathematical exercise">mathematical exercises</a> like "5&nbsp;−&nbsp;3", even if one knows the mathematics required to solve the problem. Known as <a href="/wiki/Word_problem_(mathematics_education)" title="Word problem (mathematics education)">word problems</a>, they are used in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics_education" title="Mathematics education">mathematics education</a> to teach students to connect real-world situations to the abstract language of mathematics. </p>
   <p>In general, to use mathematics for solving a real-world problem, the first step is to construct a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical model</a> of the problem. This involves abstraction from the details of the problem, and the modeller has to be careful not to lose essential aspects in translating the original problem into a mathematical one. After the problem has been solved in the world of mathematics, the <a href="/wiki/Solution_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Solution (mathematics)">solution</a> must be translated back into the context of the original problem. </p>
   <p>By outward seeing, there is various <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> from simple to <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex</a> in the real world. The some of that have also the complex mechanism with microscopic observation whereas they have the simple outward look. It depend to the <a href="/wiki/Scale_(ratio)" title="Scale (ratio)">scale</a> of the observation and the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/stability" class="extiw" title="wikt:stability">stability</a> of the mechanism. There is not only the case that simple phenomenon explained by the simple model, but also the case that simple model might be able to explain the complex phenomenon. One of example model is a model by the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a>. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abstract_problems">Abstract problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abstract problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Abstract mathematical problems arise in all fields of mathematics. While mathematicians usually study them for their own sake, by doing so results may be obtained that find application outside the realm of mathematics. <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">Theoretical physics</a> has historically been, and remains, a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Artistic_inspiration" title="Artistic inspiration">inspiration</a>. </p>
   <p>Some abstract problems have been rigorously proved to be unsolvable, such as <a href="/wiki/Squaring_the_circle" title="Squaring the circle">squaring the circle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trisecting_the_angle" class="mw-redirect" title="Trisecting the angle">trisecting the angle</a> using only the <a href="/wiki/Compass_and_straightedge_constructions" class="mw-redirect" title="Compass and straightedge constructions">compass and straightedge constructions</a> of classical geometry, and solving the general <a href="/wiki/Quintic_equation" class="mw-redirect" title="Quintic equation">quintic equation</a> algebraically. Also provably unsolvable are so-called <a href="/wiki/Undecidable_problem" title="Undecidable problem">undecidable problems</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Halting_problem" title="Halting problem">halting problem</a> for <a href="/wiki/Turing_machine" title="Turing machine">Turing machines</a>. </p>
   <p>Many abstract problems can be solved routinely, others have been solved with great effort, for some significant inroads have been made without having led yet to a full solution, and yet others have withstood all attempts, such as <a href="/wiki/Goldbach%27s_conjecture" title="Goldbach's conjecture">Goldbach's conjecture</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Collatz_conjecture" title="Collatz conjecture">Collatz conjecture</a>. Some well-known difficult abstract problems that have been solved relatively recently are the <a href="/wiki/Four-colour_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Four-colour theorem">four-colour theorem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem" title="Fermat's Last Theorem">Fermat's Last Theorem</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture" title="Poincaré conjecture">Poincaré conjecture</a>. </p>
   <p>The all of mathematical new ideas which develop a new horizon on our <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imagination</a> not correspond to the real world. Science is a way of seeking only new mathematics, if all of that correspond.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> On the view of modern mathematics, It have thought that to solve a mathematical problem be able to reduced <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mathematics#formalism" title="Philosophy of mathematics">formally</a> to an operation of symbol that restricted by the certain rules like <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Shogi" title="Shogi">shogi</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Go_(game)" title="Go (game)">go</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> On this meaning, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a> interpret the mathematics to a <i><a href="/wiki/Language_game_(philosophy)" title="Language game (philosophy)">language game</a></i> (<a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sprachspiel" class="extiw" title="de:Sprachspiel">de:Sprachspiel</a>). So a mathematical problem that <i>not</i> relation to real problem is proposed or attempted to solve by mathematician. And it may be that <i>interest</i> of studying mathematics for the mathematician himself (or herself) made much than <a href="/wiki/Novelty" title="Novelty">newness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Difference_(philosophy)" title="Difference (philosophy)">difference</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Value_judgment" title="Value judgment">value judgment</a> of the mathematical work, if mathematics is a game. <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Popper</a> criticize such viewpoint that is able to accepted in the mathematics but not in other science subjects. </p>
   <p><a href="/wiki/Computers" class="mw-redirect" title="Computers">Computers</a> do not need to have a sense of the motivations of mathematicians in order to do what they do.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-4">[4]</a></sup> Formal definitions and computer-checkable <a href="/wiki/Deductive_reasoning" title="Deductive reasoning">deductions</a> are absolutely central to <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Mathematical science">mathematical science</a>. The vitality of computer-checkable, symbol-based methodologies is not inherent to the rules alone, but rather depends on our imagination.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaton_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaton-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation">Degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathematical_problem&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Mathematics educators using <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a> for evaluation have an issue phrased by Alan H. Schoenfeld: </p> 
   <dl>
    <dd>
     How can one compare test scores from year to year, when very different problems are used? (If similar problems are used year after year, teachers and students will learn what they are, students will practice them: problems become 
     <a href="/wiki/Exercise_(mathematics)" title="Exercise (mathematics)">exercises</a>, and the test no longer assesses problem solving).
     <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
    </dd>
   </dl> 
   <p>The same issue was faced by <a href="/wiki/Sylvestre_Lacroix" class="mw-redirect" title="Sylvestre Lacroix">Sylvestre Lacroix</a> almost two centuries earlier: </p> 
   <dl>
    <dd>
     ... it is necessary to vary the questions that students might communicate with each other. Though they may fail the exam, they might pass later. Thus distribution of questions, the variety of topics, or the answers, risks losing the opportunity to compare, with precision, the candidates one-to-another.
     <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
    </dd>
   </dl> 
   <p>Such degradation of problems into exercises is characteristic of mathematics in history. For example, describing the preparations for the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Mathematical_Tripos" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge Mathematical Tripos">Cambridge Mathematical Tripos</a> in the 19th century, Andrew Warwick wrote: </p> 
   <dl>
    <dd>
     ... many families of the then standard problems had originally taxed the abilities of the greatest mathematicians of the 18th century.
     <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
    </dd>
   </dl>
