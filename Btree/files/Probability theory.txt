   <p><b>Probability theory</b> is the branch of <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> concerned with <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a>. Although there are several different <a href="/wiki/Probability_interpretations" title="Probability interpretations">probability interpretations</a>, probability theory treats the concept in a rigorous mathematical manner by expressing it through a set of <a href="/wiki/Axioms_of_probability" class="mw-redirect" title="Axioms of probability">axioms</a>. Typically these axioms formalise probability in terms of a <a href="/wiki/Probability_space" title="Probability space">probability space</a>, which assigns a <a href="/wiki/Measure_(mathematics)" title="Measure (mathematics)">measure</a> taking values between 0 and 1, termed the <a href="/wiki/Probability_measure" title="Probability measure">probability measure</a>, to a set of outcomes called the <a href="/wiki/Sample_space" title="Sample space">sample space</a>. Any specified subset of these outcomes is called an <a href="/wiki/Event_(probability_theory)" title="Event (probability theory)">event</a>. </p>
   <p>Central subjects in probability theory include discrete and continuous <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Probability_distributions" class="mw-redirect" title="Probability distributions">probability distributions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stochastic_process" title="Stochastic process">stochastic processes</a>, which provide mathematical abstractions of <a href="/wiki/Determinism" title="Determinism">non-deterministic</a> or uncertain processes or measured <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a> that may either be single occurrences or evolve over time in a random fashion. </p>
   <p>Although it is not possible to perfectly predict random events, much can be said about their behavior. Two major results in probability theory describing such behaviour are the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_large_numbers" title="Law of large numbers">law of large numbers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" title="Central limit theorem">central limit theorem</a>. </p>
   <p>As a mathematical foundation for <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, probability theory is essential to many human activities that involve quantitative analysis of data.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Methods of probability theory also apply to descriptions of complex systems given only partial knowledge of their state, as in <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>. A great discovery of twentieth-century <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> was the probabilistic nature of physical phenomena at atomic scales, described in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_probability">History of probability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of probability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
    Main article: 
    <a href="/wiki/History_of_probability" title="History of probability">History of probability</a>
   </div> 
   <p>The mathematical theory of <a href="/wiki/Probability" title="Probability">probability</a> has its roots in attempts to analyze <a href="/wiki/Game_of_chance" title="Game of chance">games of chance</a> by <a href="/wiki/Gerolamo_Cardano" title="Gerolamo Cardano">Gerolamo Cardano</a> in the sixteenth century, and by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat" title="Pierre de Fermat">Pierre de Fermat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blaise_Pascal" title="Blaise Pascal">Blaise Pascal</a> in the seventeenth century (for example the "<a href="/wiki/Problem_of_points" title="Problem of points">problem of points</a>"). <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">Christiaan Huygens</a> published a book on the subject in 1657<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and in the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">Pierre Laplace</a> completed what is today considered the classic interpretation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>Initially, probability theory mainly considered <em>discrete</em> events, and its methods were mainly <a href="/wiki/Combinatorics" title="Combinatorics">combinatorial</a>. Eventually, <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_analysis" title="Mathematical analysis">analytical</a> considerations compelled the incorporation of <em>continuous</em> variables into the theory. </p>
   <p>This culminated in modern probability theory, on foundations laid by <a href="/wiki/Andrey_Nikolaevich_Kolmogorov" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov">Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov</a>. Kolmogorov combined the notion of <a href="/wiki/Sample_space" title="Sample space">sample space</a>, introduced by <a href="/wiki/Richard_von_Mises" title="Richard von Mises">Richard von Mises</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Measure_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Measure theory">measure theory</a> and presented his <a href="/wiki/Kolmogorov_axioms" class="mw-redirect" title="Kolmogorov axioms">axiom system</a> for probability theory in 1933. This became the mostly undisputed <a href="/wiki/Axiom_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Axiom system">axiomatic basis</a> for modern probability theory; but, alternatives exist, such as the adoption of finite rather than countable additivity by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_de_Finetti" title="Bruno de Finetti">Bruno de Finetti</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Most introductions to probability theory treat discrete probability distributions and continuous probability distributions separately. The measure theory-based treatment of probability covers the discrete, continuous, a mix of the two, and more. </p> 
   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Consider an experiment that can produce a number of outcomes. The set of all outcomes is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Sample_space" title="Sample space">sample space</a></i> of the experiment. The <i><a href="/wiki/Power_set" title="Power set">power set</a></i> of the sample space (or equivalently, the event space) is formed by considering all different collections of possible results. For example, rolling an honest die produces one of six possible results. One collection of possible results corresponds to getting an odd number. Thus, the subset {1,3,5} is an element of the power set of the sample space of die rolls. These collections are called <i>events</i>. In this case, {1,3,5} is the event that the die falls on some odd number. If the results that actually occur fall in a given event, that event is said to have occurred. </p>
   <p>Probability is a <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">way of assigning</a> every "event" a value between zero and one, with the requirement that the event made up of all possible results (in our example, the event {1,2,3,4,5,6}) be assigned a value of one. To qualify as a <a href="/wiki/Probability_distribution" title="Probability distribution">probability distribution</a>, the assignment of values must satisfy the requirement that if you look at a collection of mutually exclusive events (events that contain no common results, e.g., the events {1,6}, {3}, and {2,4} are all mutually exclusive), the probability that any of these events occurs is given by the sum of the probabilities of the events.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>The probability that any one of the events {1,6}, {3}, or {2,4} will occur is 5/6. This is the same as saying that the probability of event {1,2,3,4,6} is 5/6. This event encompasses the possibility of any number except five being rolled. The mutually exclusive event {5} has a probability of 1/6, and the event {1,2,3,4,5,6} has a probability of 1, that is, absolute certainty. </p>
   <p>When doing calculations using the outcomes of an experiment, it is necessary that all those <a href="/wiki/Elementary_event" title="Elementary event">elementary events</a> have a number assigned to them. This is done using a <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variable</a>. A random variable is a function that assigns to each elementary event in the sample space a <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real number</a>. This function is usually denoted by a capital letter.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the case of a die, the assignment of a number to a certain elementary events can be done using the <a href="/wiki/Identity_function" title="Identity function">identity function</a>.    <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measure-theoretic_probability_theory">Measure-theoretic probability theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Measure-theoretic probability theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

   <p>When it's convenient to work with a dominating measure, the <a href="/wiki/Radon-Nikodym_theorem" class="mw-redirect" title="Radon-Nikodym theorem">Radon-Nikodym theorem</a> is used to define a density as the Radon-Nikodym derivative of the probability distribution of interest with respect to this dominating measure. Discrete densities are usually defined as this derivative with respect to a <a href="/wiki/Counting_measure" title="Counting measure">counting measure</a> over the set of all possible outcomes. Densities for <a href="/wiki/Absolutely_continuous" class="mw-redirect" title="Absolutely continuous">absolutely continuous</a> distributions are usually defined as this derivative with respect to the <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">Lebesgue measure</a>. If a theorem can be proved in this general setting, it holds for both discrete and continuous distributions as well as others; separate proofs are not required for discrete and continuous distributions. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_probability_distributions">Classical probability distributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classical probability distributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
    Main article: 
    <a href="/wiki/Probability_distributions" class="mw-redirect" title="Probability distributions">Probability distributions</a>
   </div> 
   <p>Certain random variables occur very often in probability theory because they well describe many natural or physical processes. Their distributions, therefore, have gained <i>special importance</i> in probability theory. Some fundamental <i>discrete distributions</i> are the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_distribution_(discrete)" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniform distribution (discrete)">discrete uniform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_distribution" title="Bernoulli distribution">Bernoulli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binomial_distribution" title="Binomial distribution">binomial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negative_binomial_distribution" title="Negative binomial distribution">negative binomial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poisson_distribution" title="Poisson distribution">Poisson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geometric_distribution" title="Geometric distribution">geometric distributions</a>. Important <i>continuous distributions</i> include the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_distribution_(continuous)" title="Uniform distribution (continuous)">continuous uniform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">normal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exponential_distribution" title="Exponential distribution">exponential</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_distribution" title="Gamma distribution">gamma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beta_distribution" title="Beta distribution">beta distributions</a>. </p>

   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_of_large_numbers">Law of large numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Law of large numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">
    Main article: 
    <a href="/wiki/Law_of_large_numbers" title="Law of large numbers">Law of large numbers</a>

   <p>Common intuition suggests that if a fair coin is tossed many times, then <i>roughly</i> half of the time it will turn up <i>heads</i>, and the other half it will turn up <i>tails</i>. Furthermore, the more often the coin is tossed, the more likely it should be that the ratio of the number of <i>heads</i> to the number of <i>tails</i> will approach unity. Modern probability theory provides a formal version of this intuitive idea, known as the <em>law of large numbers</em>. This law is remarkable because it is not assumed in the foundations of probability theory, but instead emerges from these foundations as a theorem. Since it links theoretically derived probabilities to their actual frequency of occurrence in the real world, the law of large numbers is considered as a pillar in the history of statistical theory and has had widespread influence.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>

   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_limit_theorem">Central limit theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Probability_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Central limit theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
