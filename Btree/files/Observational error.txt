   <p><b>Observational error</b> (or <b>measurement error</b>) is the difference between a <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measured</a> value of a quantity and its true value.<sup id="cite_ref-Dodge_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dodge-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, an error is not a "mistake". Variability is an inherent part of the results of measurements and of the measurement process. </p>
   <p>Measurement errors can be divided into two components: <i>random error</i> and <i>systematic error</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>Random errors are <a href="/wiki/Error" title="Error">errors</a> in measurement that lead to measurable values being inconsistent when repeated measurements of a <a href="/wiki/Time-invariant" class="mw-redirect" title="Time-invariant">constant</a> attribute or <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">quantity</a> are taken. Systematic errors are errors that are not determined by chance but are introduced by an <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">inaccuracy</a> (involving either the observation or measurement process) inherent to the <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Systematic error may also refer to an error with a non-zero <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">effect</a> of which is not reduced when <a href="/wiki/Observations" class="mw-redirect" title="Observations">observations</a> are <a href="/wiki/Averaged" class="mw-redirect" title="Averaged">averaged</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_experiments">Science and experiments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Science and experiments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>When either <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">randomness</a> or uncertainty modeled by <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a> is attributed to such errors, they are "errors" in the sense in which that term is used in <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Errors_and_residuals_in_statistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Errors and residuals in statistics">errors and residuals in statistics</a>. </p>
   <p>Every time we repeat a measurement with a sensitive instrument, we obtain slightly different results. The common <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">statistical model</a> used is that the error has two additive parts: </p> 
   <ol>
    <li><b>Systematic error</b> which always occurs, with the same value, when we use the instrument in the same way and in the same case</li> 
    <li><b>Random error</b> which may vary from observation to another.</li>
   </ol> 
   <p>Systematic error is sometimes called <b>statistical bias</b>. It may often be reduced with standardized procedures. Part of the learning process in the various <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a> is learning how to use standard instruments and protocols so as to minimize systematic error. </p>
   <p>Random error (or <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variation</a>) is due to factors which cannot or will not be controlled. Some possible reason to forgo controlling for these random errors is because it may be too expensive to control them each time the experiment is conducted or the measurements are made. Other reasons may be that whatever we are trying to measure is changing in time (see <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynamic model">dynamic models</a>), or is fundamentally probabilistic (as is the case in quantum mechanics — see <a href="/wiki/Measurement_in_quantum_mechanics" title="Measurement in quantum mechanics">Measurement in quantum mechanics</a>). Random error often occurs when instruments are pushed to the extremes of their operating limits. For example, it is common for digital balances to exhibit random error in their least significant digit. Three measurements of a single object might read something like 0.9111g, 0.9110g, and 0.9112g. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Random_errors_versus_systematic_errors">Random errors versus systematic errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Random errors versus systematic errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Measurement errors can be divided into two components: random error and systematic error.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
   <p><b>Random error</b> is always present in a measurement. It is caused by inherently unpredictable fluctuations in the readings of a measurement apparatus or in the experimenter's interpretation of the instrumental reading. Random errors show up as different results for ostensibly the same repeated measurement. They can be estimated by comparing multiple measurements, and reduced by averaging multiple measurements. </p>
   <p><b>Systematic error</b> is predictable and typically constant or proportional to the true value. If the cause of the systematic error can be identified, then it usually can be eliminated. Systematic errors are caused by imperfect calibration of measurement instruments or imperfect methods of <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a>, or interference of the <a href="/wiki/Surroundings" title="Surroundings">environment</a> with the measurement process, and always affect the results of an <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiment</a> in a predictable direction. Incorrect zeroing of an instrument leading to a zero error is an example of systematic error in instrumentation. </p>
   <p>The Performance Test Standard PTC 19.1-2005 “Test Uncertainty”, published by the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME), discusses systematic and random errors in considerable detail. In fact, it conceptualizes its basic uncertainty categories in these terms. Random error can be caused by unpredictable fluctuations in the readings of a measurement apparatus, or in the experimenter's interpretation of the instrumental reading; these fluctuations may be in part due to interference of the environment with the measurement process. The concept of random error is closely related to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">precision</a>. The higher the precision of a measurement instrument, the smaller the variability (<a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a>) of the fluctuations in its readings. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_systematic_error">Sources of systematic error<span id="Systematic"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sources of systematic error">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperfect_calibration">Imperfect calibration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imperfect calibration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Sources of systematic error may be imperfect calibration of measurement instruments (zero error), changes in the <a href="/wiki/Biophysical_environment" title="Biophysical environment">environment</a> which interfere with the measurement process and sometimes imperfect methods of <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observation</a> can be either zero error or percentage error. If you consider an experimenter taking a reading of the time period of a pendulum swinging past a <a href="/wiki/Fiducial_marker" title="Fiducial marker">fiducial marker</a>: If their stop-watch or timer starts with 1 second on the clock then all of their results will be off by 1 second (zero error). If the experimenter repeats this experiment twenty times (starting at 1 second each time), then there will be a <a href="/wiki/Percentage_error" class="mw-redirect" title="Percentage error">percentage error</a> in the calculated average of their results; the final result will be slightly larger than the true period. </p>
   <p><a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">Distance</a> measured by <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> will be systematically overestimated if the slight slowing down of the waves in air is not accounted for. Incorrect zeroing of an instrument leading to a zero error is an example of systematic error in instrumentation. </p>
   <p>Systematic errors may also be present in the result of an <a href="/wiki/Computational_mechanics" title="Computational mechanics">estimate</a> based upon a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical model</a> or <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical law">physical law</a>. For instance, the estimated <a href="/wiki/Oscillation_frequency" class="mw-redirect" title="Oscillation frequency">oscillation frequency</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a> will be systematically in error if slight movement of the support is not accounted for. </p> 
   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantity">Quantity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quantity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Systematic errors can be either constant, or related (e.g. proportional or a percentage) to the actual value of the measured quantity, or even to the value of a different quantity (the reading of a <a href="/wiki/Ruler" title="Ruler">ruler</a> can be affected by environmental temperature). When it is constant, it is simply due to incorrect zeroing of the instrument. When it is not constant, it can change its sign. For instance, if a thermometer is affected by a proportional systematic error equal to 2% of the actual temperature, and the actual temperature is 200°, 0°, or −100°, the measured temperature will be 204° (systematic error = +4°), 0° (null systematic error) or −102° (systematic error = −2°), respectively. Thus the temperature will be overestimated when it will be above zero, and underestimated when it will be below zero. </p> 
   <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drift">Drift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Drift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
   <p>Systematic errors which change during an experiment (<a href="/wiki/Stochastic_drift" title="Stochastic drift">drift</a>) are easier to detect. Measurements indicate trends with time rather than varying randomly about a <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a>. Drift is evident if a measurement of a <a href="/wiki/Constant_(mathematics)" title="Constant (mathematics)">constant</a> quantity is repeated several times and the measurements drift one way during the experiment. If the next measurement is higher than the previous measurement as may occur if an instrument becomes warmer during the experiment then the measured quantity is variable and it is possible to detect a drift by checking the zero reading during the experiment as well as at the start of the experiment (indeed, the <a href="/wiki/Vernier_scale" title="Vernier scale">zero reading</a> is a measurement of a constant quantity). If the zero reading is consistently above or below zero, a systematic error is present. If this cannot be eliminated, potentially by resetting the instrument immediately before the experiment then it needs to be allowed by subtracting its (possibly time-varying) value from the readings, and by taking it into account while assessing the accuracy of the measurement. </p>
   <p>If no pattern in a series of repeated measurements is evident, the presence of fixed systematic errors can only be found if the measurements are checked, either by measuring a known quantity or by comparing the readings with readings made using a different apparatus, known to be more accurate. For example, if you think of the timing of a pendulum using an accurate <a href="/wiki/Stopwatch" title="Stopwatch">stopwatch</a> several times you are given readings randomly distributed about the mean. A systematic error is present if the stopwatch is checked against the '<a href="/wiki/Speaking_clock" title="Speaking clock">speaking clock</a>' of the telephone system and found to be running slow or fast. Clearly, the pendulum timings need to be corrected according to how fast or slow the stopwatch was found to be running. </p>
   <p>Measuring instruments such as <a href="/wiki/Ammeter" title="Ammeter">ammeters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voltmeter" title="Voltmeter">voltmeters</a> need to be checked periodically against known standards. </p>
   <p>Systematic errors can also be detected by measuring already known quantities. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a> fitted with a <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">diffraction grating</a> may be checked by using it to measure the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the D-lines of the <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> which are at 600&nbsp;nm and 589.6&nbsp;nm. The measurements may be used to determine the number of lines per millimetre of the diffraction grating, which can then be used to measure the wavelength of any other spectral line. </p>
   <p>Constant systematic errors are very difficult to deal with as their effects are only observable if they can be removed. Such errors cannot be removed by repeating measurements or averaging large numbers of results. A common method to remove systematic error is through <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">calibration</a> of the measurement instrument. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_random_error">Sources of random error<span id="Random"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sources of random error">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>The random or stochastic error in a measurement is the error that is random from one measurement to the next. Stochastic errors tend to be <a href="/wiki/Normal_distribution" title="Normal distribution">normally distributed</a> when the stochastic error is the sum of many independent random errors because of the <a href="/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" title="Central limit theorem">central limit theorem</a>. Stochastic errors added to a regression equation account for the variation in <i>Y</i> that cannot be explained by the included <i>X</i>s. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveys">Surveys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Surveys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>The term "Observational error" is also sometimes used to refer to response errors and some other types of <a href="/wiki/Non-sampling_error" title="Non-sampling error">non-sampling error</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dodge_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dodge-1">[1]</a></sup> In survey-type situations, these errors can be mistakes in the collection of data, including both the incorrect recording of a response and the correct recording of a respondent's inaccurate response. These sources of non-sampling error are discussed in Salant and Dillman (1995)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and Bland and Altman (1996).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>These errors can be random or systematic. Random errors are caused by unintended mistakes by respondents, interviewers and/or coders. Systematic error can occur if there is a systematic reaction of the respondents to the method used to formulate the survey question. Thus, the exact formulation of a survey question is crucial, since it affects the level of measurement error (<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>). Different tools are available for the researchers to help them decide about this exact formulation of their questions, for instance estimating the quality of a question using <a href="/wiki/Multitrait-multimethod_matrix" title="Multitrait-multimethod matrix">MTMM experiments</a> or predicting this quality using the Survey Quality Predictor software (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://sqp.upf.edu">SQP</a>). This information about the quality can also be used in order to <a href="/wiki/Correction_for_attenuation" title="Correction for attenuation">correct for measurement error</a> (<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>) </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_regression_analysis">Effect on regression analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Observational_error&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on regression analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>If the <a href="/wiki/Dependent_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Dependent variable">dependent variable</a> in a regression is measured with error, regression analysis and associated hypothesis testing are unaffected, except that the <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_determination" title="Coefficient of determination">R<sup>2</sup></a> will be lower than it would be with perfect measurement. </p>
   <p>However, if one or more <a href="/wiki/Independent_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent variable">independent variables</a> is measured with error, then the regression coefficients and standard <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypothesis test">hypothesis tests</a> are invalid.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p. 187</span></sup> </p>
