  <div class="mw-parser-output">
   <p>In <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, the <b>Burgers vortex</b> is an exact solution to the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a> governing <a href="/wiki/Viscous_flow" class="mw-redirect" title="Viscous flow">viscous flow</a>. The Burgers vortex describes a stationary, <a href="/wiki/Self-similarity" title="Self-similarity">self-similar</a> flow. An inward, radial flow, tends to concentrate <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> in a narrow column around the symmetry axis. At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> diffusion tends to spread the vorticity. The stationary Burgers vortex arises when the two effects balance. </p>
   <p>The Burgers vortex, apart from serving as an illustration of the <a href="/wiki/Vortex_stretching" title="Vortex stretching">vortex stretching</a> mechanism, may describe such flows as tornados, where the vorticity is provided by continuous <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>-driven vortex stretching. </p>
