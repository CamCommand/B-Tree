   <p><b>William Allen</b> (December 18 or 27, 1803 – July 11, 1879) was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Democratic_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Democratic Party">Democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Representative</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Governors of Ohio">31st Governor of Ohio</a>. </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_family">Early life and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Allen_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Allen was born in <a href="/wiki/Edenton,_North_Carolina" title="Edenton, North Carolina">Edenton, North Carolina</a> and moved to <a href="/wiki/Chillicothe,_Ohio" title="Chillicothe, Ohio">Chillicothe, Ohio</a> in 1819, after his parents' death. He was of <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-OHC_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHC-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>Allen and his sister Mary Granberry Allen lived in Chillicothe together. His sister married Reverend Pleasant Thurman, and their son, <a href="/wiki/Allen_G._Thurman" title="Allen G. Thurman">Allen G. Thurman</a>, followed in his uncle's footsteps, becoming a lawyer and politician. </p>
   <p>Allen attended Chillicothe Academy before studying law with <a href="/wiki/Edward_King_(Ohio_politician)" title="Edward King (Ohio politician)">Colonel Edward King</a>. He was <a href="/wiki/Admission_to_the_bar_in_the_United_States" title="Admission to the bar in the United States">admitted to the bar</a> in Ohio at age 21.<sup id="cite_ref-OHC_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHC-1">[1]</a></sup> He began his career as a politician in the Democratic Party at a young age.<sup id="cite_ref-ryan_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-2">[2]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Allen_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <div class="thumb tleft">
    <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
     <a href="/wiki/File:Fruit_Hill.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Fruit_Hill.png/200px-Fruit_Hill.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Fruit_Hill.png/300px-Fruit_Hill.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Fruit_Hill.png/400px-Fruit_Hill.png 2x" data-file-width="918" data-file-height="548"></a> 
     <div class="thumbcaption">
      <div class="magnify">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Fruit_Hill.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
      </div>Fruit Hill (home of 
      <a href="/wiki/Duncan_McArthur" title="Duncan McArthur">McArthur</a> and Allen
     </div>
    </div>
   </div> 
   <p>Allen served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Representative" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Representative">United States Representative</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> from 1833 to 1835, losing his bid for re-election. He served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senator">United States Senator</a> from Ohio from 1837 to 1849, losing a bid for a third term in 1848. </p>
   <p>While in the Senate, Allen was one of a group of Western Democrat <a href="/wiki/Expansionism" title="Expansionism">expansionists</a> who asserted that the U.S. had a valid claim to the entire <a href="/wiki/Oregon_boundary_dispute" title="Oregon boundary dispute">Oregon Country</a>, which was an issue during the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1844" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 1844">1844 U.S. presidential election</a>. He suggested that the United States should be prepared to go to war with the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a> in order to annex the entire Oregon Country up to <a href="/wiki/Russian_America" title="Russian America">Russian-owned Alaska</a> at <a href="/wiki/Parallel_54%C2%B040%E2%80%B2_north" title="Parallel 54°40′ north">latitude 54°40′N</a>. This position ultimately produced the slogan "<a href="/wiki/Fifty-Four_Forty_or_Fight!" class="mw-redirect" title="Fifty-Four Forty or Fight!">Fifty-Four Forty or Fight!</a>," coined in 1846 by opponents of such a policy (not, as popularly believed, a slogan in the 1844 Presidential campaign).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Allen supported "popular sovereignty" and the presidential candidacy of fellow-Democrat <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Cass" title="Lewis Cass">Lewis Cass</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1848" class="mw-redirect" title="United States presidential election, 1848">1848</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OHC_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHC-1">[1]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>In 1849, Allen retired to his farm, "Fruit Hill", which had belonged to his father-in-law, and fellow Ohio Governor, <a href="/wiki/Duncan_McArthur" title="Duncan McArthur">Duncan McArthur</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ryan_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-2">[2]</a></sup> near <a href="/wiki/Chillicothe,_Ohio" title="Chillicothe, Ohio">Chillicothe, Ohio</a>. Allen identified himself as a "<a href="/wiki/Peace_Democrat" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace Democrat">Peace Democrat</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_the_American_Civil_War" title="Opposition to the American Civil War">opposing</a> the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OHC_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OHC-1">[1]</a></sup> Allen did not return to public service for nearly a quarter century, until he served as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Governor of Ohio">Governor of Ohio</a> from 1874 to 1876. He unsuccessfully sought a second two-year term in an 1875 election. </p>
   <p>Allen was noted for his loud voice. A friend asked Senator <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Tappan" title="Benjamin Tappan">Benjamin Tappan</a> if a fellow Ohioan was still in Washington. Tappan replied "No, he left yesterday and is probably by this time in <a href="/wiki/Cumberland,_Maryland" title="Cumberland, Maryland">Cumberland, Maryland</a>, but if you will go to Bill Allen and tell him to raise that window and call him he will come back."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Allen_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>At the close of his administration, he retired to private life at Fruit Hill, where he died in 1879.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Allen is buried at <a href="/wiki/Grandview_Cemetery,_Chillicothe" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandview Cemetery, Chillicothe">Grandview Cemetery, Chillicothe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Allen_(governor)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p><a href="/wiki/Allen_County,_Kansas" title="Allen County, Kansas">Allen County, Kansas</a> is named for William Allen.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>In 1887, Ohio donated a <a href="/wiki/William_Allen_(Niehaus)" title="William Allen (Niehaus)">statue of Allen</a> to the <a href="/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall_Collection" title="National Statuary Hall Collection">National Statuary Hall Collection</a>, which was exhibited in the <a href="/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall" title="National Statuary Hall">National Statuary Hall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U.S. Capitol</a>. The statue was sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_H._Niehaus" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles H. Niehaus">Charles H. Niehaus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_Historical_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Ohio Historical Society">Ohio Historical Society</a> held a statewide poll on the suitability of Allen as a distinguished representative of the state. The poll found that many Ohioans objected to Allen. On August 26, the Ohio National Statuary Committee voted to replace Allen's statue with a statue of Ohio-born inventor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ohio_General_Assembly" title="Ohio General Assembly">Ohio General Assembly</a> agreed to replace the statue in part because "Allen’s pro-slavery position and outspoken criticism of President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a> make him a poor representative for Ohio in the U.S. Capitol."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, lack of funding for the Edison statue delayed replacement of the Allen statue.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Edison statue was completed in spring 2015, and was installed on September 20, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The statue of Allen was relocated to the Ross County Heritage Center in Chillicothe.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> </p>
