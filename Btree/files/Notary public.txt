   <p><b><a href="/wiki/Notary_public" title="Notary public">Notaries public</a></b> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> are commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_of_New_York" title="Secretary of State of New York">Secretary of State of New York</a> after passing a short examination in <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Legal_procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal procedure">procedure</a> and submitting an application for appointment accompanied by the proper fees.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A notary's commission is received from and kept on file with the <a href="/wiki/County_clerk" class="mw-redirect" title="County clerk">county clerk</a> of the county in which they reside or do business, but notaries are empowered to actually perform their duties anywhere in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>

   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Requirements">Requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Notary_public_(New_York)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>Notaries public must be residents of the state <i>or</i> have an office or place of business in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-Exec_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exec-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Attorney_at_law_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney at law (United States)">Attorneys at law</a> With the exception of taking and passing the initial, written notary examination, New York bar members must follow the same appointment and re-appointment process as non-lawyers; lawyers are not automatically appointed as notaries because they are licensed as lawyers.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup> Someone who is a commissioner or inspector of elections may also be a notary.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> County clerk employees may be appointed a notary without submitting the customary fees; however, the written examination is required.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A state legislator may also be a notary, without any conflict of interest.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Married_Women%27s_Property_Acts_in_the_United_States" title="Married Women's Property Acts in the United States">Married women's property acts</a> in the mid-19th century, a married woman can be a notary.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>Certain persons <i>cannot</i> be commissioned as a notary public in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup> These include a non-citizen,<sup id="cite_ref-Exec_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exec-3">[3]</a></sup> someone who has neither a residence nor a business in New York,<sup id="cite_ref-Exec_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exec-3">[3]</a></sup> a person convicted of certain felonies,<sup id="cite_ref-Exec_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exec-3">[3]</a></sup> a person removed from office as notary or commissioner of deeds,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and a convicted draft dodger.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The State Constitution prohibits (the) elected Sheriffs from holding any other office; <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriffs</a> may not be appointed notaries.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Const_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Const-11">[11]</a></sup> The collateral consequences of certain criminal conduct may be relieved upon the issuance of a certificate of good conduct by the parole board or an executive pardon by the governor. </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers">Powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Notary_public_(New_York)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>New York notaries are empowered to administer <a href="/wiki/Oath" title="Oath">oaths</a> and <a href="/wiki/Affirmation_in_law" title="Affirmation in law">affirmations</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_office" title="Oath of office">oaths of office</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> to take <a href="/wiki/Affidavit" title="Affidavit">affidavits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(law)" title="Deposition (law)">depositions</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> to receive and certify acknowledgments or proofs (of execution) of deeds, mortgages and powers of attorney and other instruments in writing.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Notaries may also demand acceptance or payment of foreign and inland bills of exchange, promissory notes and obligations in writing.<sup id="cite_ref-DOS_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOS-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Other powers include required attendance at the forced opening of an abandoned safe deposit box to observe the opening and conduct and provide a written inventory of the contents.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> </p>
   <p>New York notaries may not issue certified copies of publicly recordable documents (i.e. birth certificate, marriage certificate, bankruptcy discharge, divorce decree, etc.); notably (as emphasized by official publications) they may <i>not</i> certify copies of documents (for instance, "I hereby certify that this is a true and correct copy...," is beyond the authority of a New York notary). However, a notary may issue a notarial copy certification in which the document custodian signs and swears to the authenticity of the original and facsimile document. New York notaries, including attorney-notaries, may not solemnize marriages in a civil ceremony. In New York state, a notary is not required for a testator to write or execute a last will and testament. New York law requires two witnesses, who do not need to be notaries, to attend and observe the will signing ceremony; subsequent to the execution by both testator and subscribing witnesses, these witnesses may then swear to the mental competence of the testator and other factors associated with the execution ceremony. A notary may, upon request, take a "self-proving affidavit" or an "affidavit of execution" sworn to and signed by the testator and the two (subscribing) witnesses, which will serve as formal proof and testimony of the proper will execution in the probate process at the county surrogate..<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seal_or_stamp">Seal or stamp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Notary_public_(New_York)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Seal or stamp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>New York notaries are not ordered to possess or use official marking stamps or seals of any kind. In lieu of a traditional inked stamp, New York notaries must handwrite, typewrite or print, below the official signature, the "statement of authority": commissioned name; the words "Notary Public State of New York"; the county in which they are qualified (the county in which the county clerk records a signed and sworn oath of office); commission expiration date. Most New York notaries possess and use some form of marking stamp and/or official embossing seal. It is considered a best practice, however, for a professional notary to incorporate the use of a marking inked stamp and official embossing seal..<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> </p> 
   <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notary_Commission_Appointment_and_Re-Appointment">Notary Commission Appointment and Re-Appointment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Notary_public_(New_York)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notary Commission Appointment and Re-Appointment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
   <p>In New York, a notary commission is granted for a four-year term. Other than the pre-qualification written examination, no continuing education is required during the term. Both initial and re-appointment applications require a $60.00 application fee ($40.00 for the appointment and $20.00 for the oath of office filing). The application for re-appointment is sent by US Mail to every notary approximately three months prior to the term expiration. No re-examination is required if the application for re-appointment is returned during the six-month "grace period" after the date of expiration; a twelve-month "grace period" extends from the date of discharge of a member of the United States military. .<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> </p> 
   <pre>JANE DOE
Notary Public, State of New York
Qualified in Albany County
Commission Expires December 31, 20XX
</pre> 
   <p><i>The customary "statement of authority" marking stamp data for placement below the signature of the official signature of a notary;</i> however, this information may be handwritten, typed or printed beneath the signature of a notary - a stamp is not required.<i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></i> </p>
   <p><br> Notaries may collect $2.00 to administer an oath or affirmation (but not an oath of office) and to take an acknowledgment or proof of execution. Many notaries elect to waive the fee, especially if working within the employ of a private or public employer.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Each county clerk also has a notary on duty in the clerk's office to serve the public at no charge. Travel expense charged is a separate fee, unregulated by law, and is privately negotiated between the notary and client. </p>
   <p>As of April, 2017, New York State had approximately 291,000 commissioned notaries public. </p>
   <p>Cities in the State of New York appoint and commission an officer called a Commissioner of Deeds, which can be applied for with the respective City Clerk's office and is appointed by the City Council. These local officials possess limited powers and the acts performed by them may not be accepted in some states or international destinations. </p>
